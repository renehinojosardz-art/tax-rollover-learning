Founder Rollover Triage

Lesson 0002 · M&A rollover and contribution memo drafting

This lesson teaches one memo skill: classify a founder rollover before calling it tax-free. Your first draft should identify the bucket, not just describe the economics.

Skill target: Given a short rollover fact pattern, write the first five sentences of a tax-structure memo: classification, intended mechanics, principal authorities, assumptions, and diligence risks.

Working Scenario

Founder owns target equity. Buyer wants Founder to sell 70% for cash and roll 30% into the buyer-side platform. The platform may be a partnership/LLC, a corporation, or a newly formed holding structure.

Founder
sells 70%
Buyer
rolls 30%
Holdco / Platform

The First Distinction

Ask what the rollover equity is going into. That answer usually sends the memo to one of two first-order regimes:

Partnership-side rollover

Likely starting point: section 721.

Section 721 generally provides nonrecognition when property is contributed to a partnership in exchange for a partnership interest. In M&A, the danger is that the step may not be a clean contribution if cash, debt shifts, distributions, or side payments are part of the same plan.

Pull: section 721, section 707, section 752, IRS Pub. 541.

Corporate-side rollover

Likely starting point: section 351 or section 368.

Section 351 generally covers property transferred to a corporation solely for stock when the transferor group controls the corporation immediately after. A C-corp recap or Type F reorg may require separate section 368 analysis before the sale or rollover step.

Pull: section 351, section 368, Treas. Reg. 1.368-2.

Memo Drafting Move

For a first-pass memo, do not write "Founder rolls 30% tax-free" as the conclusion. Write a classification paragraph:

The proposed rollover should first be classified by the tax status of the buyer-side vehicle receiving Founder's retained equity. If Founder contributes property to an LLC taxed as a partnership in exchange for a partnership interest, section 721 is the likely starting point, subject to disguised-sale, liability-shift, and distribution analysis. If Founder contributes property to a corporation in exchange for stock, section 351 is the likely starting point, subject to control, boot, liability, and related reorganization questions. If a pre-closing recapitalization or Type F reorganization is used, section 368 and the applicable regulations should be analyzed before treating the later sale and rollover steps as settled.

Five Questions Before You Draft

  1. Recipient: Is rollover equity issued by an LLC taxed as a partnership, a corporation, or an entity whose classification is changing?
  2. Property: Is Founder contributing target stock, LLC interests, partnership interests, real estate, operating assets, or cash?
  3. Consideration: Is Founder receiving only equity, or also cash, notes, preferred rights, guarantees, or other property?
  4. Liabilities: Are debt or guarantees shifting as part of the rollover?
  5. Sequencing: Is there a pre-closing F reorg, recapitalization, conversion, merger, or contribution step?

Practice

Scenario: Founder contributes 30% of her target LLC interests to Buyer Holdco LLC, which is taxed as a partnership, and receives Buyer Holdco units. In the same closing, Buyer pays Founder cash for the remaining 70%. The draft memo says, "The rollover should be tax-free under section 351."

Choose the best memo edit.

Model Answer

A better first-pass memo edit would say:

Because the rollover equity is issued by an LLC taxed as a partnership, the rollover should be analyzed first under section 721 rather than section 351. The memo should separately analyze the taxable cash sale of the 70% interest and should flag whether the cash sale, rollover contribution, liability shifts, or related distributions create disguised-sale or other recognition concerns under Subchapter K.
